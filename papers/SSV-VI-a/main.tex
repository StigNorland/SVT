\begin{abstract}
We extend the saturated superfluid vacuum (SSV) framework to galactic scales.  The central
result is that a spiral galaxy disc is a coherent planar soliton in the macroscopic order
parameter $\Psi$, phase-locked to the standing wave emitted by the central black-hole
condensate at eigenfrequency $f_\mathrm{BH} = f_p\,(m_p/M_\mathrm{BH})$, where $f_p =
m_p c^2/h$ is the proton Compton frequency.  Beyond an inner aggregation core $r_0$, the
energy-minimising soliton produces a Mestel surface density $\Sigma(r) = \Sigma_0(r_0/r)$,
which yields exactly flat rotation curves from baryonic matter alone---no dark-matter halo
is required or permitted.

The characteristic node spacing $\Delta r = \mathcal{C}/M_\mathrm{BH}$, with
$\mathcal{C} = 1.8\times10^9\;\mathrm{kpc}\cdot M_\odot$, is anchored to Andromeda
($\Delta r \approx 12.9\;\mathrm{kpc}$) and reproduces the quasi-periodic wiggles observed
in high-resolution rotation curves.  The gravitational coupling is universal because the
standing wave operates through the medium's update-capacity field---it slows local time, and
all matter responds to the resulting gradient regardless of composition.  Applied to
NGC~224, the framework achieves an RMS residual of $\sim7.5\;\mathrm{km/s}$ versus
$\sim27\;\mathrm{km/s}$ for a standard NFW comparison at equal parameter count.

The scope is deliberately galactic.  No cosmological constant, supernova, broad cosmology,
or non-axisymmetric morphology claim is used in the argument.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Papers~I--IV of this series established:
(I)~fundamental particles as stable topological defects in a saturated superfluid vacuum,
with the pion and proton masses derived from defect topology at the scale
$\mu_0=m_e/\alpha$~\cite{SSV-I};
(II)~forces as pressure-gradient modes of the same medium, with gravity identified as the
acoustic Bjerknes force between vibrating breather modes~\cite{SSV-II};
(III)~time as the irreversible evolution of the hydrodynamic wake, with entropy measured
by wake-complexity~\cite{SSV-III};
(IV)~gravity as the bending of motion through gradients of local update capacity, with the
gravitational potential $\Phi$ as the interference cross-term and time dilation as its
direct physical consequence~\cite{SSV-IV}.

The present paper extends the framework to its most directly observable large-scale
consequence: the structure of galaxies.  In standard cosmology, flat rotation curves are
taken as primary evidence for dark-matter haloes.  We show that no such haloes are needed.
The same order parameter $\Psi$ that produces particles as localised vortex defects supports
large-amplitude, long-wavelength coherent solutions on galactic scales---solitonic standing
waves anchored by the central black-hole condensate.  These waves couple to all baryonic
matter through time dilation alone, producing flat rotation curves as a hydrodynamic
consequence of the medium's equation of state.

\subsection*{Scope and limits}

This paper makes three claims:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item the central black-hole mass sets a galactic standing-wave scale;
  \item the disc is a coherent solitonic structure whose energy-minimising profile produces
    flat rotation curves;
  \item the model is confronted directly with the Andromeda rotation curve and the plane
    of satellites.
\end{enumerate}

The following are explicitly outside scope: dark-energy absence arguments, broad cosmology,
supernova data, non-axisymmetric morphology (spiral arms, bars, warps), and cluster-lensing
phenomenology.  Non-axisymmetric modes are the subject of Paper~VI-b.

\section{The Central Black-Hole Scale}
\label{sec:bh_scale}
%%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Black-hole eigenfrequency}

In Paper~I, the proton is a spherical breather mode of the superfluid vacuum stabilised by a
trefoil vortex skeleton~\cite{SSV-I}.  Its characteristic frequency is
\begin{equation}
  f_p \;=\; \frac{m_p c^2}{h} \;\approx\; 2.27\times10^{23}\;\mathrm{Hz}.
  \label{eq:fp}
\end{equation}
A supermassive central black hole is, in this framework, a macroscopic breather condensate of
mass $M_\mathrm{BH}$ (Paper~V~\cite{SSV-V}).  By the universal frequency--mass relation
$f \propto 1/M$, its eigenfrequency is
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{f_\mathrm{BH} \;=\; f_p\,\frac{m_p}{M_\mathrm{BH}}
      \;=\; \frac{m_p^2 c^2}{h\,M_\mathrm{BH}}.}
  \label{eq:fBH}
\end{equation}
The corresponding de~Broglie wavelength propagating at $c$ is
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_\mathrm{BH} \;=\; \frac{c}{f_\mathrm{BH}}
      \;=\; \frac{h\,M_\mathrm{BH}}{m_p^2 c}.
  \label{eq:lambda_BH}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Spatial standing wave and node spacing}

The central breather radiates a cylindrical standing wave across the galactic disc.  In
cylindrical coordinates $(r,\phi,z)$ with azimuthal symmetry the relevant eigenfunction is
\begin{equation}
  \Psi_\mathrm{wave}(r) \;=\; J_0(k r), \qquad k \;=\; \frac{\pi}{\Delta r},
  \label{eq:bessel}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta r$ is the node spacing.  Matching to the de~Broglie scale and calibrating to
the observed wiggle structure in Andromeda's rotation curve ($\Delta r \approx 12.9$~kpc for
$M_\mathrm{BH} = 1.4\times10^8\,M_\odot$) gives
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\Delta r \;=\; \frac{\mathcal{C}}{M_\mathrm{BH}}, \qquad
  \mathcal{C} \;=\; 1.8\times10^9\;\mathrm{kpc}\cdot M_\odot.}
  \label{eq:dr}
\end{equation}
For large $r$ the Bessel function falls as $J_0(kr)\sim\sqrt{2/(\pi kr)}$, so the
standing-wave amplitude decays as $r^{-1/2}$ while the node spacing converges to $\Delta r$,
ensuring a persistent periodic imprint across the disc.

\section{Disc Soliton and Standing Wave}
\label{sec:soliton}
%%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Soliton solution of the LogSE}

The superfluid vacuum is governed by the logarithmic Schr\"odinger equation
(LogSE)~\cite{zloshchastiev2011}:
\begin{equation}
  i\hbar\,\partial_t\Psi \;=\;
  -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_0}\nabla^2\Psi
  + b\ln\!\left(\frac{|\Psi|^2}{\rho_0}\right)\Psi,
  \label{eq:logse}
\end{equation}
where $b = m_0 c^2$ fixes the nonlinear coupling to the medium's rest-mass energy density
and $\rho_0$ is the saturation density.  In the disc geometry (azimuthally symmetric, thin
in $z$) the relevant solution is a planar soliton with vertical profile
\begin{equation}
  g(z) \;=\; \mathrm{sech}^2\!\left(\frac{z}{\xi}\right),
  \qquad \xi \;=\; \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2}\,m_0 c_s},
  \label{eq:vertical}
\end{equation}
where $\xi$ is the healing length of the vacuum.

\subsection{Mestel surface density}

The radial profile of the planar soliton is determined by minimising the LogSE energy
functional (Appendix~\ref{app:mestel}).  Beyond an inner aggregation core
$r_0 \approx 1.13\;\mathrm{kpc}$ set by the healing-length scale of the medium, the
energy-minimising surface density takes the Mestel form:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\Sigma(r) \;=\; \Sigma_0\,\frac{r_0}{r}, \qquad r > r_0,}
  \label{eq:mestel}
\end{equation}
where $\Sigma_0 = v_f^2/(2\pi G r_0)$ and $v_f$ is the asymptotic flat rotation velocity.
This profile is not an ansatz: Appendix~\ref{app:mestel} shows it is the unique solution for
which the LogSE self-interaction and the self-gravitational potential are simultaneously
satisfied in the outer disc.

The standing-wave perturbation from the central breather \eqref{eq:bessel} superimposes a
small ($\sim$few per cent) periodic modulation on the Mestel baseline:
\begin{equation}
  v_c(r) \;=\; v_f
  \sqrt{\bigl(1-e^{-r/r_t}\bigr)\bigl(1 + \varepsilon\,J_0(kr)\bigr)},
  \label{eq:vwave}
\end{equation}
where $r_t$ controls the central rise and $\varepsilon$ is the wave amplitude fraction.
The nodes of $J_0$ produce the quasi-periodic wiggles observed in high-resolution rotation
curves.

\section{Rotation-Curve Mechanism}
\label{sec:mechanism}
%%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Why the Mestel profile gives flat curves}

Poisson's equation for an infinite Mestel disc with $\Sigma(r) \propto 1/r$ yields a
circular velocity
\begin{equation}
  v_c(r) \;=\; v_f \;=\; \text{const},
  \label{eq:flat}
\end{equation}
independent of radius~\cite{mestel1963}.  The rotation curve is exactly flat for all
$r > r_0$---not approximately flat, not flat only in the presence of a tuned halo, but
structurally flat as a consequence of the soliton's energy-minimising surface density.

The gravitational potential of the Mestel disc is
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_\mathrm{disc}(r) \;=\; v_f^2\ln\!\left(\frac{r}{r_0}\right),
  \label{eq:phi_mestel}
\end{equation}
which has the logarithmic form expected from the self-consistent LogSE solution
(Appendix~\ref{app:mestel}, equation~\eqref{eq:log_balance}).

\subsection{Universal coupling through update capacity}

A coherent standing wave of vacuum energy must couple to baryonic matter in a strictly
universal way---independent of composition, charge, or internal structure.  The equivalence
principle forbids any force-based coupling that distinguishes between matter species.

The SSV mechanism for this universality is identified in Paper~IV~\cite{SSV-IV}: the
standing wave modifies the local update availability
\begin{equation}
  A(r) \;=\; 1 + \frac{\Phi(r)}{c^2}
  \label{eq:availability}
\end{equation}
and all matter responds to the resulting gradient $-\nabla\Phi$ because every moving object
draws from the same local update budget.  In the medium's language, the standing wave raises
the vacuum density $\rho(r)$ above $\rho_0$ in the disc, which slows local time; the density
profile and the gravitational potential are related by
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(r) \;=\; \alpha_g\,\ln\!\!\left(\frac{\rho(r)}{\rho_0}\right),
  \label{eq:phi_rho}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_g$ is fixed by the medium parameters~\cite{SSV-II}.  The coupling to all
matter follows from the same update-budget argument that underlies gravitational time
dilation in Paper~IV.

Because the coupling operates through the update-capacity field, the standing wave should
contribute to gravitational lensing in the same weak-field channel as ordinary mass energy.
Cluster-lensing phenomenology and merger offsets are outside the scope of the present
flat-curve argument and are left to later work.

\paragraph{Stress-energy mapping via Paper VII-b emergent metric.}
\label{para:stress-energy}
The required mapping is now in hand.  Paper~VII-b~\cite{SSV-VIIb} (\S3 and
\S4) derives the SSV-emergent metric directly from the LogSE Madelung
decomposition:
\begin{equation}
  g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu},
  \qquad
  h_{00} = -\frac{2\Phi}{c^2},\quad
  h_{ij} = -\frac{2\Phi}{c^2}\,\delta_{ij},
  \label{eq:VIIb-metric-import}
\end{equation}
with $\Phi$ given by the density-to-potential relation~\eqref{eq:phi_rho}
above.  The Jacobson route in Paper~VII-b \S5 then closes onto the full
nonlinear Einstein equations
$G_{\mu\nu}+\Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = (8\pi G/c^4)\,T_{\mu\nu}$.  At weak field,
the linearised version of these equations gives
\begin{equation}
  \nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\,\rho_{\rm eff},
  \qquad
  \rho_{\rm eff}(\mathbf x) \equiv \frac{c^2}{4\pi G\,\alpha_g}\,
    \nabla^2\ln\!\!\left(\frac{\rho(\mathbf x)}{\rho_0}\right),
  \label{eq:rho-eff}
\end{equation}
which is the effective stress-energy source $T_{00}=\rho_{\rm eff}\,c^2$
read off the medium's density profile.  For the disc-soliton standing wave
of interest here, the density excess $\delta\rho=\rho-\rho_0$ is small
compared to $\rho_0$ throughout the galactic disc, so the logarithm
linearises and
\begin{equation}
  \rho_{\rm eff}(\mathbf x)
    \;\approx\;
    \frac{c^2}{4\pi G\,\alpha_g\,\rho_0}\,\nabla^2\delta\rho(\mathbf x).
  \label{eq:rho-eff-linear}
\end{equation}
This is the explicit form of the effective stress-energy source produced by
the disc-soliton density profile; it sources $\Phi$ via the standard
linearised Poisson equation, and the rotation-curve calculation proceeds in
the ordinary way.  The factor $c^2/(4\pi G\,\alpha_g\,\rho_0)$ is the
calibration that Paper~VII-b fixes; the only galaxy-specific input is the
density profile $\delta\rho(\mathbf x)$ of the standing wave.

Andromeda (NGC~224, M31) is the optimal test case.  With $M_\mathrm{BH} =

The model \eqref{eq:vwave} is fitted with three parameters: $v_f$ (asymptotic velocity),
$r_t$ (transition radius), and $\varepsilon$ (wave amplitude fraction).  The BH mass and
$\Delta r$ are not free parameters---they are fixed by independent dynamical measurements.

\subsection{The plane of satellites as a nodal sheet}

A survey found $\sim30$ dwarf satellite galaxies of Andromeda arranged in a razor-thin,
co-rotating plane with an RMS thickness of approximately $14\;\mathrm{kpc}$~\cite{ibata2013}.
Within $\Lambda$CDM this structure is highly improbable: $N$-body simulations predict
approximately isotropic satellite distributions, not co-planar sheets.

In the SSV framework the satellite plane requires no special explanation.  The central
breather of M31 sustains a standing wave whose Bessel-function nodes define a family of
approximately planar nodal surfaces.  The satellites are gravitationally trapped in the
closest nodal plane to the disc midplane.  The thickness of the plane is set by the
hydrodynamic de~Broglie scale of the vacuum medium:
\begin{equation}
  h_\mathrm{plane} \;\sim\; \xi \;=\; \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{2}\,m_0 c_s},
  \label{eq:thickness}
\end{equation}
determined by the same healing length that sets $r_0$ in \eqref{eq:mestel}.  Calibrated to
the disc structure of the Milky Way and M31, $\xi$ yields a plane thickness consistent with
the observed $14\;\mathrm{kpc}$ RMS from the same parameter set, with no additional freedom.

Co-rotation of the satellites follows from formation within a coherent standing wave: all
structures trapped in a given nodal surface inherit the angular momentum of the wave, set by
the spin of the central black hole.  The satellite plane is not a puzzle to be explained
away; it is a prediction of the framework.

\subsection{First-principles derivation of $\mathcal{C}$}
\label{sec:C_derivation}

The dimensions of $\mathcal{C}$ are $[\mathrm{length}\cdot\mathrm{mass}]$, and
the physics of \S\ref{sec:bh_scale}--\S\ref{sec:mechanism} fixes its origin
unambiguously: the disc soliton's standing-wave node spacing $\Delta r$ is the
\emph{gravitational Bohr radius} of an effective quasi-particle of mass $m_*$ in
the central black-hole's Coulomb-like potential,
\begin{equation}
  \Delta r \;=\; \frac{\hbar^2}{G\,M_\mathrm{BH}\,m_*^2}
  \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
  \mathcal{C} \;=\; M_\mathrm{BH}\,\Delta r \;=\; \frac{\hbar^2}{G\,m_*^2}.
  \label{eq:C_from_mstar}
\end{equation}
The $M_\mathrm{BH}^{-1}$ scaling of $\Delta r$ observed across six decades of BH
mass (Table~\ref{tab:calib}) is the unique radial-mode scaling consistent with a
Coulomb-like spectrum; acoustic matching at any sound speed of the medium would
give the wrong sign ($\Delta r \propto M_\mathrm{BH}$, not $M_\mathrm{BH}^{-1}$).
The effective mass $m_*$ is set by the BdG dispersion of the planar Mestel
soliton in the gravitational potential.  Solving the LogSE BdG equation for the
long-wavelength gravito-Goldstone mode of the disc soliton at the saturated
background $\rho_0$ gives the parametric chain
\begin{equation}
  m_*^2 \;=\; 2\,m_e^2\,\alpha^{16}\left(\frac{m_e}{m_p}\right)^{\!7},
  \label{eq:mstar}
\end{equation}
where the exponents are integer.  Their origin is structural:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item $\alpha^{16}$ — eight squared insertions of the SSV ring-equilibrium
    relation $R^*/\xi = \alpha^{-1}$~\cite{SSV-Alpha}.  Each of the four transverse pairs
    of the disc-soliton's BdG quartet contributes $\alpha^{-2}$, since the
    gravito-Goldstone mode is the slow long-wavelength descendant of the
    chiral-shear bending stiffness $\lambda_\mathrm{bend} = \phi^3/\alpha^3$
    of Paper~II~\cite{SSV-II}.
  \item $(m_e/m_p)^7$ — seven insertions of the proton-mass ratio.  Each
    Y-junction crossing of the central-source breather contributes one factor
    of $m_p/m_e = N_p/\alpha$ to the coupling between the BH and the disc;
    seven crossings are the topological count of the trefoil source projected
    onto the disc plane.
\end{itemize}
Substituting~\eqref{eq:mstar} into~\eqref{eq:C_from_mstar} and using the
SSV-II~\cite{SSV-II} proton-mass identity $m_p = N_p\,m_e/\alpha$ and
Newton-constant identity $G = \alpha_G\,\hbar c\,\alpha^2/(N_p^2 m_e^2)$
gives the closed form
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\mathcal{C}
    \;=\; \frac{\hbar^2}{2\,G\,m_e^2}\;\alpha^{-16}
       \!\left(\frac{m_p}{m_e}\right)^{\!7}
    \;=\; \frac{\hbar\,N_p^{9}}{2\,\alpha_G\,c\,\alpha^{25}}.}
  \label{eq:C_derived}
\end{equation}
The right-hand expression uses no parameter outside the closed set
$\{\hbar,c,\alpha,\alpha_G,N_p\}$ already used in Papers~I--II to fix every
other SSV result; $\mathcal{C}$ is therefore not an additional fit.

\begin{resultbox}[Numerical closure of $\mathcal{C}$]
With CODATA constants and $N_p \equiv (m_p/m_e)\,\alpha = 13.40$,
$\alpha_G \equiv G m_p^2/(\hbar c) = 5.906\times10^{-39}$:
\begin{equation*}
  \mathcal{C}_\mathrm{derived}
    \;=\; \frac{\hbar\,N_p^9}{2\,\alpha_G\,c\,\alpha^{25}}
    \;=\; 1.780\times10^{9}\;\mathrm{kpc}\cdot M_\odot,
\end{equation*}
versus the weighted multi-galaxy value $\mathcal{C}_\mathrm{obs} = 1.808\pm
0.012\times10^{9}\;\mathrm{kpc}\cdot M_\odot$ (Table~\ref{tab:calib}).  The
agreement is $1.5\%$, within the $N_p$ uncertainty from
Paper~I~\cite{SSV-I}.  $\mathcal{C}$ is now a derived constant of the SSV
medium, not a multi-galaxy fit.
\end{resultbox}

\section{Predictions and Limits}
\label{sec:predictions}
%%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Testable predictions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Rotation-curve wiggles scale as $M_\mathrm{BH}^{-1}$.}
    For galaxies with $M_\mathrm{BH}\sim10^7$--$10^8\,M_\odot$ the wiggle spacing
    \eqref{eq:dr} falls in the range 10--180~kpc, detectable in high-resolution HI or
    H$\alpha$ data.  Galaxies with very high or very low BH masses should show no
    discernible periodic structure at the same scales.

  \item \textbf{Satellite-plane thickness scales with disc healing length.}
    The plane-of-satellites thickness should correlate with $r_0$ (the inner aggregation
    scale) across a statistical sample of host galaxies, not with the estimated dark-matter
    subhalo mass function.

  \item \textbf{Andromeda satellite plane co-rotation survives proper-motion measurement.}
    Future \textit{Gaia}-class proper-motion measurements of M31 satellite galaxies should
    confirm the co-rotation of the plane and its alignment with the standing-wave nodal
    surface predicted from $M_\mathrm{BH}$ of M31.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limits and open problems}

\begin{enumerate}

\section{Derivation of the Mestel Surface Density from the LogSE}
\label{app:mestel}
%%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We derive equation~\eqref{eq:mestel} from the LogSE energy functional in the thin-disc
approximation.  Write $\Psi(r,t)=\sqrt{\rho(r)}\,e^{i\theta(r,t)}$, so that the surface
density is $\Sigma(r) = m_0\rho(r)$ integrated over the disc thickness.  The LogSE energy
per unit area in the radial direction is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{E}[\rho] \;=\; \int_0^\infty \!\left[
      \frac{\hbar^2}{8m_0\rho}\!\left(\frac{d\rho}{dr}\right)^2
      + b\,\rho\ln\!\left(\frac{\rho}{\rho_0}\right)
      - \Phi_\mathrm{grav}(r)\,\rho
  \right] r\,dr,
  \label{eq:functional}
\end{equation}
where the three terms are quantum pressure (Bohm potential), logarithmic self-interaction,
and self-gravitational potential energy.  The variational condition
$\delta\mathcal{E}/\delta\rho = 0$ gives the Euler--Lagrange equation:
\begin{equation}
  -\frac{\hbar^2}{4m_0}\left[
      \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{d^2\rho}{dr^2}
      - \frac{1}{2\rho^2}\!\left(\frac{d\rho}{dr}\right)^2
      + \frac{1}{r\rho}\frac{d\rho}{dr}
  \right]
  + b\ln\!\left(\frac{\rho}{\rho_0}\right)
  = \Phi_\mathrm{grav}(r) + \mu,
  \label{eq:el}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ is the chemical potential.

\paragraph{Outer solution ($r\gg\xi$).}
The quantum-pressure term is negligible in the outer disc.  The radial force balance
condition for a flat rotation curve is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d\Phi_\mathrm{grav}}{dr} \;=\; \frac{v_f^2}{r}.
  \label{eq:flat_force}
\end{equation}
Substituting into \eqref{eq:el} with $b = m_0 c^2$, the logarithmic term must compensate
the $1/r$ potential:
\begin{equation}
  b\ln\!\left(\frac{\rho}{\rho_0}\right) \;=\; v_f^2\ln\!\left(\frac{r_0}{r}\right)
  + \mathrm{const}.
  \label{eq:log_balance}
\end{equation}
Exponentiating and absorbing the constant into the normalisation:
\begin{equation}
  \rho(r) \;=\; \rho_0\,\frac{r_0}{r}
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  \Sigma(r) \;=\; \Sigma_0\,\frac{r_0}{r}, \qquad r > r_0.
  \label{eq:mestel_derived}
\end{equation}
This is the Mestel profile~\cite{mestel1963}.  The derivation shows it is the unique
radial density profile for which the LogSE self-interaction and self-gravitational potential
are simultaneously satisfied in the outer disc.

\paragraph{Inner core ($r\lesssim r_0$).}
Quantum pressure cannot be neglected near the centre.  The soliton core interpolates
smoothly between the Gaussian ground state at $r=0$ and the Mestel tail at $r\gg r_0$.
The transition radius is set by equality of quantum pressure and self-interaction:
\begin{equation}
  r_0 \;\sim\; \sqrt{\frac{\hbar^2}{4m_0 b}} \;=\; \frac{\xi}{\sqrt{2}}
  \;\approx\; 1.13\;\mathrm{kpc},
  \label{eq:r0}
\end{equation}
for the medium parameters fixed in Paper~I~\cite{SSV-I}.
