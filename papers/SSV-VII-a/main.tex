\begin{abstract}
This paper develops the quantum-mechanical limit of the Saturated Superfluid
Vacuum (SSV) framework. Its scope is deliberately narrow: the Madelung
transformation, the linear Schrodinger limit, uncertainty as a hydraulic
localisation bound, and measurement as a physical reconnection channel. The
formal result is the recovery of the Schrodinger equation from the low-amplitude
hydrodynamic regime. The interpretive result is a proposed physical reading of
quantisation, localisation limits, and measurement in the same medium. Claims
about emergent geometry, Einstein equations, cosmogony, and full-series synthesis
are left to companion papers.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The SSV programme treats the vacuum as a saturated superfluid order parameter
rather than as an empty background. In earlier papers, the same medium supports
topological defects, hydrodynamic forces, and irreversible wake formation. The
present paper asks a narrower question: under what conditions does ordinary
quantum mechanics appear as the low-amplitude hydrodynamic limit of that medium?

The working thesis is that quantum behaviour is not an additional foundational
axiom. It is the wave-mechanical regime of the order parameter when perturbations
are small, gradients are gentle, and defects can be treated through an effective
potential. In that regime the Madelung variables map the complex wave equation
to continuity plus Euler-type flow equations, and the inverse map recovers the
Schrodinger equation for a slowly varying envelope.

This paper separates three claim levels.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{C\arabic*.}]
    \item \textbf{Formal derivation.} The Madelung transformation and its inverse
    show how a Schrodinger-type equation is equivalent to hydrodynamic flow with
    a quantum-pressure term.

    \item \textbf{Controlled limit.} In the low-amplitude, long-wavelength regime,
    the nonlinear medium terms can be absorbed into background phase and
    effective potentials, leaving the ordinary linear Schrodinger equation.

    \item \textbf{Physical interpretation.} Quantisation, uncertainty, and
    measurement are read as circulation topology, localisation stiffness, and
    irreversible reconnection respectively. These are model commitments rather
    than independent mathematical theorems.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Madelung Form of the Medium}
\label{sec:madelung}

Let the local state of the saturated medium be represented by a complex order
parameter
\begin{equation}
    \Psi(\mathbf{x},t)=\sqrt{\rho(\mathbf{x},t)}\,e^{iS(\mathbf{x},t)/\hbar},
    \label{eq:polar}
\end{equation}
where $\rho$ is the density and $S$ is the phase action. The associated velocity
field is
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{v}=\frac{1}{m}\nabla S.
    \label{eq:velocity}
\end{equation}

For a Gross--Pitaevskii/logarithmic-Schrodinger-type medium, the local envelope
obeys a wave equation of the schematic form
\begin{equation}
    i\hbar\,\partial_t\Psi =
    \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V_{\rm ext} + U(\rho)\right]\Psi,
    \label{eq:medium_wave}
\end{equation}
where $V_{\rm ext}$ is a slowly varying effective potential and $U(\rho)$ is the
medium self-interaction. The specific equation of state belongs to the earlier
SSV papers; the present paper needs only the hydrodynamic structure common to
this class of order-parameter equations.

Substituting Eq.~(\ref{eq:polar}) into Eq.~(\ref{eq:medium_wave}) and separating
real and imaginary parts gives
\begin{align}
    \partial_t\rho+\nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v}) &= 0,
    \label{eq:continuity}\\
    \partial_t S+\frac{(\nabla S)^2}{2m}+V_{\rm ext}+U(\rho)+Q &= 0,
    \label{eq:hamilton_jacobi}
\end{align}
with
\begin{equation}
    Q(\mathbf{x},t)=
    -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\nabla^2\sqrt{\rho}}{\sqrt{\rho}}.
    \label{eq:Q}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, taking a gradient of Eq.~(\ref{eq:hamilton_jacobi}) gives an
Euler equation for the flow:
\begin{equation}
    m\left(\partial_t+\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla\right)\mathbf{v}
    =-\nabla\left(V_{\rm ext}+U(\rho)+Q\right).
    \label{eq:euler}
\end{equation}

Equations~(\ref{eq:continuity})--(\ref{eq:euler}) are the Madelung equations:
compressible hydrodynamics with an additional quantum-pressure contribution.
This is the formal bridge used in both directions. Starting from the wave
equation gives hydrodynamics; starting from the hydrodynamic pair plus the
irrotational velocity condition reconstructs the wave equation.

\section{Schrodinger Limit}
\label{sec:schrodinger}

Write the density as $\rho=\rho_0+\delta\rho$, with
$|\delta\rho|\ll\rho_0$, and assume gradients are long compared with the core or
healing scale of the medium. In this regime the self-interaction can be expanded
around the saturated background:
\begin{equation}
    U(\rho)=U(\rho_0)+U'(\rho_0)\delta\rho+\cdots .
    \label{eq:U_expand}
\end{equation}
The constant term shifts the global phase. The linear term is either negligible
for a sufficiently weak perturbation or can be collected into an effective
potential. The slowly varying envelope $\psi$ then obeys
\begin{equation}
    i\hbar\,\partial_t\psi =
    \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2+V_{\rm eff}\right]\psi,
    \label{eq:schrodinger}
\end{equation}
which is the ordinary Schrodinger equation for the effective degree of freedom
under study.

The controlled claim is therefore not that all quantum theory has been derived
in one step. The claim is narrower: whenever the SSV medium is in a
low-amplitude, long-wavelength regime with a well-defined envelope, the same
hydrodynamic variables reduce to the linear Schrodinger dynamics used in
ordinary quantum mechanics.

\section{Quantisation as Circulation Topology}
\label{sec:quantisation}

The phase in Eq.~(\ref{eq:polar}) must be single-valued around any closed loop
that does not cross a zero of $\Psi$. Therefore
\begin{equation}
    \oint_C \nabla S\cdot d\mathbf{l}=2\pi n\hbar,
    \qquad n\in\mathbb{Z}.
    \label{eq:phase_quantisation}
\end{equation}
Using Eq.~(\ref{eq:velocity}), this becomes the circulation condition
\begin{equation}
    \oint_C \mathbf{v}\cdot d\mathbf{l}=\frac{2\pi n\hbar}{m}.
    \label{eq:circulation}
\end{equation}

In this reading, quantisation is tied to the topology of the phase field. It is
not inserted as a separate rule for allowed orbits; it is the consequence of a
single-valued order parameter with vortex cores acting as excluded phase
singularities. The detailed spectrum of any bound system still depends on its
effective potential and boundary conditions, but the origin of discrete winding
numbers is hydrodynamic.

\section{Uncertainty as a Hydraulic Bound}
\label{sec:uncertainty}

The usual uncertainty relation follows mathematically from Fourier analysis of
wave packets:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta x\,\Delta p\geq\frac{\hbar}{2}.
    \label{eq:uncertainty}
\end{equation}
SSV adds a physical interpretation of the same relation. A perturbation
localised to a short length scale must include large gradients of
$\sqrt{\rho}$ and phase. Through Eq.~(\ref{eq:Q}), those gradients carry a
quantum-pressure cost. Pushing $\Delta x$ downward therefore forces a larger
spread in momentum modes and a larger hydraulic stiffness penalty.

This section should not be read as a replacement proof of the uncertainty
principle. The proof remains the standard wave-packet theorem. The SSV claim is
that the theorem has a medium-level interpretation: localisation is limited
because the order parameter cannot be squeezed arbitrarily without exciting the
pressure and phase-gradient channels of the fluid.

\section{Measurement as Reconnection}
\label{sec:measurement}

The measurement argument is deliberately kept narrow. A measurement is treated
as a physical interaction that correlates a microscopic degree of freedom with a
macroscopic apparatus by driving some part of the combined medium into an
irreversible reconnection or wake-writing event.

Before measurement, a superposition is represented by a coherent field
configuration,
\begin{equation}
    \Psi=\sum_k c_k\psi_k .
    \label{eq:superposition}
\end{equation}
As long as the relevant branches remain phase-coherent, interference terms are
available. A measurement interaction is the regime in which the probe, apparatus,
and surrounding medium cross a threshold where topology changes, phonons are
emitted, or persistent wake degrees of freedom are written. Reversing the event
would require restoring the original phase relation across all of those
channels, not merely evolving a small isolated wave packet backward.

\subsection{Born Weights as a Working Model}

The natural branch weights in Eq.~(\ref{eq:superposition}) are $|c_k|^2$ because
the local energy and intensity available to drive an apparatus response are
quadratic in the wave amplitude. This gives the usual Born form
\begin{equation}
    P_k=|c_k|^2.
    \label{eq:born}
\end{equation}
At the present stage this is best stated as a consistency argument, not a full
derivation. The missing calculation is a reconnection-threshold model showing
that the basin volume for outcome $k$ is proportional to the incoming branch
intensity under realistic apparatus coupling.

\subsection{What This Does and Does Not Claim}

This picture does not require consciousness, an external classical realm, or a
new postulate of collapse. It also does not yet derive a complete theory of
detector statistics. Its useful claim is more modest: SSV supplies a candidate
physical channel for irreversibility during measurement, namely topology change
and wake formation in the medium.

\section{Focused Predictions and Open Calculations}

The present paper leaves two near-term checks.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Q\arabic*.}]
    \item \textbf{Reconnection signatures in controlled measurement systems.}
    If detector clicks are accompanied by irreversible medium events, then
    sufficiently sensitive optomechanical or superconducting measurement
    platforms should show excess correlated acoustic, thermal, or phase-noise
    signatures at the transition from reversible coupling to recorded outcome.

    \item \textbf{Born-rule basin calculation.}
    A reduced reconnection-threshold model should test whether branch basin
    volumes scale as $|c_k|^2$ for simple two-channel apparatus couplings. This
    is the calculation needed before the measurement section can be promoted
    from physical interpretation to formal derivation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

SSV VII-a isolates the quantum-mechanics claim from the geometry and cosmogony
claims elsewhere in the series. The paper's formal core is the Madelung bridge
and the Schrodinger limit. Its interpretive core is that quantisation reflects
phase topology, uncertainty reflects hydraulic localisation stiffness, and
measurement reflects irreversible reconnection or wake-writing in the medium.
That is enough to make the paper standalone without asking it to carry the
Einstein-equation or cosmogony arguments.
