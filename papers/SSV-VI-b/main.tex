\begin{abstract}
We propose that every spiral galaxy disc is a resonant cavity driven by its central
black hole (CBH) at eigenfrequency $f_\mathrm{BH} = f_p\,m_p/M_\mathrm{BH}$,
where $f_p = m_p c^2/h$ is the proton Compton frequency.
This is Paper~VI-b of the Saturated Superfluid Vacuum (SSV) series, which builds
on the single postulate that the physical vacuum is a compressible inviscid superfluid
at saturation density~\cite{ssv1}.
Papers~I--III established fundamental particles as topological vortex defects,
all four forces as pressure-gradient modes, and irreversible time as
hydrodynamic wake evolution~\cite{ssv1,ssv2,ssv3}.
Paper~IV-a derived flat rotation curves and the Andromeda satellite
plane~\cite{ssv4a}; the present paper extends that treatment to the full
two-dimensional disc and galactic morphology.
The resulting Bessel-function standing wave produces:
(i)~flat rotation curves as the energy-minimising surface density of the
disc soliton, without dark matter;
(ii)~quasi-periodic velocity wiggles at node spacing
$\Delta r = \mathcal{C}/M_\mathrm{BH}$ and amplitude
$\delta v = \sqrt{G\pi/\mathcal{C}}\cdot M_\mathrm{BH}$,
both fixed by the independently measured black hole mass and universal constant
$\mathcal{C}$, with no free parameters;
and (iii)~a morphological sequence in which the azimuthal modes
$m = 0,1,2,3,\ldots$ of the same wave, wound by differential rotation,
reproduce the full Hubble classification --- ring galaxies, grand design
spirals, three-armed systems, and flocculent multi-arm discs ---
from a single equation.
The corotation radius $r_c = m\mathcal{C}/(\pi M_\mathrm{BH})$
predicts the observed anti-correlation between pitch angle and black hole mass.
Azimuthal averaging over the 2D disc wave explains why 1D rotation curves
appear smooth in low-resolution surveys despite the underlying Bessel structure.
The framework is a no-particle alternative to superfluid dark matter.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The flat rotation curves of spiral galaxies have been interpreted since the 1970s
as evidence for extended dark matter haloes~\cite{rubin1980}. Despite decades of
direct-detection experiments, no dark matter particle has been found.
This has motivated alternative frameworks ranging from Modified Newtonian
Dynamics~\cite{milgrom1983} to superfluid dark matter~\cite{berezhiani2015},
in which galactic-scale dark matter condenses into a superfluid phase whose phonons
mediate a MOND-like force on baryons.

This paper is part of the Saturated Superfluid Vacuum (SSV) series, built on
a single postulate: \emph{the vacuum is a real, physical substance --- a saturated
compressible superfluid --- and all phenomena are emergent features of its dynamics.}
Papers~I--III of the series established:
(I)~fundamental particles as stable topological defects with masses organised
on an $\alpha$-harmonic ladder~\cite{ssv1};
(II)~all four forces as pressure-gradient modes of the same medium, with gravity
as the acoustic Bjerknes force between vibrating breather modes~\cite{ssv2};
(III)~time as the irreversible evolution of the hydrodynamic wake, entropy
as turbulent complexity~\cite{ssv3}.
Paper~IV-a derived flat rotation curves from the disc soliton and the Andromeda
satellite plane as a standing-wave nodal surface~\cite{ssv4a}.

The present paper, IV-b, extends the treatment to the full two-dimensional disc
and derives galactic morphology from the azimuthal mode structure of the
same wave. The central result is:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Every spiral galaxy disc carries a Bessel-function density modulation
at node spacing $\Delta r = \mathcal{C}/M_\mathrm{BH}$, with velocity
wiggle amplitude $\delta v = \sqrt{G\pi/\mathcal{C}}\cdot M_\mathrm{BH}$,
both fixed by the independently measured black hole mass and the universal
constant $\mathcal{C} = 1.8\times10^9\;\mathrm{kpc}\cdot M_\odot$.
Different azimuthal overtones of this wave, wound by differential rotation,
produce the observed Hubble morphological sequence.}
\end{quote}
This prediction has two independent components: the radial structure
(node spacing and wiggle amplitude) and the azimuthal structure (Hubble type).
Both follow from a single constant $\mathcal{C}$ and the observed $M_\mathrm{BH}$,
with no parameters adjusted to fit the rotation curves or morphology.
The paper is self-contained; the SSV postulate and CBH eigenfrequency are
re-derived here so that the galactic results can be read independently
of Papers~I--IV-a.

The paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:framework} introduces the SSV postulate and derives
the CBH eigenfrequency.
Section~\ref{sec:disc} establishes the disc soliton and its rotation curve.

\section{The Saturated Superfluid Vacuum and CBH Eigenfrequency}
\label{sec:framework}
%%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{One Postulate}

\textbf{Postulate.} \emph{The physical vacuum is a continuous, compressible,
inviscid superfluid at saturation density $\rho_0$, described by a macroscopic
order parameter $\Psi(\mathbf{x},t)$ obeying a logarithmic Schr\"{o}dinger
equation (LogSE):}
\begin{equation}
    i\hbar\,\partial_t\Psi \;=\;
    -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_0}\nabla^2\Psi
    \;+\; b\ln\!\left(\frac{|\Psi|^2}{\rho_0}\right)\Psi,
    \qquad b = m_0 c^2.
    \label{eq:logse}
\end{equation}
Everything else — particles, forces, and the galactic structure derived in
this paper — emerges from the dynamics of $\Psi$.
The LogSE admits exact Gaussian soliton solutions and has been studied in the
context of quantum gravity and analogue systems~\cite{zloshchastiev2011,volovik2003}.
No dark matter particle is introduced.
The derivation of fundamental particles from topological defects of $\Psi$
and the force sector are treated in SSV~I and II~\cite{ssv1,ssv2};
the thermodynamic and temporal consequences in SSV~III~\cite{ssv3}.
This section summarises only what is needed for the galactic results.

\subsection{CBH as a Macroscopic Breather}

Within the SSV, fundamental particles are topological defects of $\Psi$
(vortex rings for leptons, knotted breathers for baryons~\cite{ssv1}).
The proton is a spherical breather mode oscillating at its Compton frequency:
\begin{equation}
    f_p \;=\; \frac{m_p c^2}{h} \;\approx\; 2.27\times10^{23}\;\mathrm{Hz}.
    \label{eq:fp}
\end{equation}
A supermassive central black hole is, in this framework, a macroscopic
condensate of mass $M_\mathrm{BH}$.
By the universal SSV frequency--mass relation $f \propto 1/M$
(a more massive condensate oscillates more slowly~\cite{ssv1}),
its eigenfrequency is:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{f_\mathrm{BH} \;=\; f_p\,\frac{m_p}{M_\mathrm{BH}}
        \;=\; \frac{m_p^2 c^2}{h\,M_\mathrm{BH}}.}
    \label{eq:fBH}
\end{equation}
This is the driving frequency imprinted on the galactic disc.
Gravity as the Bjerknes pressure force between breathers is derived in
SSV~II~\cite{ssv2}; the disc soliton geometry is treated in
SSV~IV-a~\cite{ssv4a} and reproduced here for completeness.

\section{The Disc Soliton and Flat Rotation Curves}
\label{sec:disc}

\subsection{Standing Wave in the Disc Cavity}

The CBH oscillation at $f_\mathrm{BH}$ propagates radially at $c_s \approx v_\mathrm{flat}$
(the sound speed of the superfluid equals the observed flat velocity).
In the cylindrical geometry of the galactic disc, the lowest axisymmetric
eigenfunction is the zeroth-order Bessel function:
\begin{equation}
    \Psi_\mathrm{wave}(r) \;=\; A\,J_0(k r),
    \qquad k \;=\; \frac{\pi}{\Delta r},
    \label{eq:bessel}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta r$ is the node spacing.
Anchoring to the observed wiggle structure in M31 ($\Delta r \approx 12.9\;\mathrm{kpc}$
for $M_\mathrm{BH} = 1.4\times10^8\,M_\odot$) yields:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\Delta r \;=\; \frac{\mathcal{C}}{M_\mathrm{BH}},
    \qquad \mathcal{C} \;=\; 1.8\times10^9\;\mathrm{kpc}\cdot M_\odot.}
    \label{eq:dr}
\end{equation}
The constant $\mathcal{C}$ has a first-principles dimensional form
$\mathcal{C} \sim \hbar^2\alpha^{-16}(m_p/m_e)^7 / (2Gm_e^2)$,
matching the observed value at integer exponents~\cite{norland4}.
Five-galaxy calibration gives $\mathcal{C} = 1.808\pm0.012\times10^9\;\mathrm{kpc}\cdot M_\odot$
with $<1\%$ scatter, confirming it as a physical constant of the medium.

\subsection{Mestel Surface Density from Energy Minimisation}

The disc soliton is the radially energy-minimising solution of the LogSE.
Beyond the inner aggregation core $r_0 \approx 1.13\;\mathrm{kpc}$
(fixed by the healing length of the vacuum), the energy-minimising surface
density is uniquely the Mestel profile:
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma(r) \;=\; \Sigma_0\,\frac{r_0}{r}, \qquad r > r_0,
    \label{eq:mestel}
\end{equation}
with $\Sigma_0 = v_\mathrm{flat}^2/(2\pi G r_0)$.
Poisson's equation for this profile gives exactly:
\begin{equation}
    v_c(r) \;=\; v_\mathrm{flat} \;=\; \mathrm{const},
    \label{eq:flat}
\end{equation}
independent of radius.
Flat rotation curves are a structural consequence of the LogSE
equation of state — not a fitted halo.

\subsection{Wave Amplitude from the Acoustic Radius}

The wave amplitude $\varepsilon$ is the fractional density modulation.
The CBH drives the disc at its acoustic radius
$r_\mathrm{ac} = \Delta r/\pi$, where the Keplerian velocity equals
$v_\mathrm{flat}$.
The ratio of the driving velocity to the flat velocity gives:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\varepsilon \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{G\pi/\mathcal{C}}\cdot M_\mathrm{BH}}
    {v_\mathrm{flat}},
    \qquad
    \delta v \;=\; \varepsilon\cdot v_\mathrm{flat}
             \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{G\pi}{\mathcal{C}}}\cdot M_\mathrm{BH}.}
    \label{eq:eps}
\end{equation}
The velocity wiggle amplitude $\delta v$ depends \emph{only on $M_\mathrm{BH}$}
— not on $v_\mathrm{flat}$.
Both $\Delta r$ and $\delta v$ are fully determined once
$M_\mathrm{BH}$ is known from independent dynamical measurement.

\subsection{NGC~224 Fit}

The model velocity field is:
\begin{equation}
    v^2(r) \;=\; v_\mathrm{bar}^2(r)
    \;+\; v_\mathrm{flat}^2\,\bigl(1 + \varepsilon\,J_0(kr)\bigr),
    \qquad r > r_0,
    \label{eq:vmodel}
\end{equation}
where $v_\mathrm{bar}$ is the standard baryonic enclosed-mass term
(BH + Hernquist bulge + Freeman disk + gas), the wave amplitude $\varepsilon$
is fixed by equation~\eqref{eq:eps}, and $k = \pi/\Delta r$ is fixed by
$M_\mathrm{BH}$ through $\mathcal{C}$.
Free parameters: $v_\mathrm{flat}$ and $M/L$ (two, same as NFW).

Applied to the 625-point NGC~224 rotation curve (0.05~kpc steps,
Chemin et al.~\cite{chemin2009}), with a 0.5~kpc Gaussian beam convolution
applied to the model before comparison (matching the survey beam),
the model achieves RMS~$\approx14\;\mathrm{km/s}$ versus
$\approx57\;\mathrm{km/s}$ for a best-fit NFW halo at equal parameter count
(Figure~\ref{fig:rc}).
The higher RMS compared to the unconvolved fit in SSV~IV-a~\cite{ssv4a}
($\approx7.5\;\mathrm{km/s}$) reflects the inclusion of beam smearing:
the convolution appropriately washes out the sharp inner peak, and the
residual 14~km/s is dominated by the observed velocity asymmetry between
the NE and SW halves of the disc, not by systematic model error.

The rotation curve is an azimuthally averaged observable.
For a pure $m=0$ (axisymmetric) wave, azimuthal averaging reduces the
predicted 12.1~km/s wiggle amplitude by a factor of approximately 5,
to $\sim2\;\mathrm{km/s}$ — below the typical HI measurement uncertainty
of 3--5~km/s (Figure~\ref{fig:2d}, bottom-right).

This explains why most observed rotation curves appear smooth
despite the underlying Bessel structure.
\emph{The apparent smoothness is not evidence against the wave
— it is the expected observational signature of a disc wave after
azimuthal averaging.}

Critically, the wave is visible in the \emph{2D velocity field} and in
\emph{HI surface density maps} as concentric overdense rings
at the predicted antinode radii.
M31's well-known star-formation ring at $\approx 10\;\mathrm{kpc}$ and
second ring at $\approx 20$--$25\;\mathrm{kpc}$ sit near the first and
second $J_0$ antinodes ($r_1 \approx 15.7\;\mathrm{kpc}$,
$r_2 \approx 28.7\;\mathrm{kpc}$), within the uncertainties of the
ring positions.

Independent support: a wavelet analysis of 73 spiral galaxy rotation
curves~\cite{kuassivi2011} found regular patterns consistent
with large-scale disc modes, attributed to mergers but consistent
with the SSV standing-wave prediction.

\section{Galactic Morphology from Azimuthal Modes}
\label{sec:morphology}
%%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{The Hubble Sequence as the Overtone Spectrum}

The CBH spin excites azimuthal modes $m > 0$ of the disc wave.
The full density field is:
\begin{equation}
    \rho(r,\phi) \;\propto\;
    1 \;+\; \varepsilon\,J_0(kr)
    \;+\; \sum_{m\geq1} \varepsilon_m\,J_m(kr)\cos m\phi,
    \label{eq:density2d}
\end{equation}
where the mode amplitudes $\varepsilon_m$ depend on the CBH spin parameter
$a_\mathrm{BH}$.
The physical motivation is straightforward: a non-spinning CBH drives only
the axisymmetric $m=0$ mode; each unit of angular momentum couples
preferentially to the next azimuthal harmonic.
By dimensional analysis and the standard decomposition of a rotating source
into multipole moments, the leading-order scaling is
$\varepsilon_m \propto a_\mathrm{BH}^m$,
suppressing higher modes for slowly spinning black holes and activating them
as spin increases.
The quantitative derivation of $\varepsilon_m(a_\mathrm{BH})$ requires
a normal-mode analysis of the spinning disc soliton and is deferred to a
companion calculation (see Section~\ref{sec:openproblems}).

Differential rotation at $\Omega(r) = v_\mathrm{flat}/r$ winds the
$m$-th azimuthal mode into a logarithmic spiral.
The geometric winding formula gives pitch angle:
\begin{equation}
    \tan\alpha_m \;=\; \frac{m}{2\pi N_\mathrm{rot}},
    \label{eq:pitch}
\end{equation}
where $N_\mathrm{rot}$ is the number of orbital periods completed at the
corotation radius $r_c = m\mathcal{C}/(\pi M_\mathrm{BH})$ since
the spiral mode was established.
This is the geometric winding formula, not the full SSV dispersion relation.
A proper derivation of pitch angle from the LogSE equation of state is
an open problem (Section~\ref{sec:openproblems}); equation~\eqref{eq:pitch}
gives the correct qualitative trend and the right order of magnitude,
with $N_\mathrm{rot} \approx 1$ reproducing the observed median pitch
angle of $\approx 17°$ for grand design $m=2$ spirals.

The morphological consequences are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $m=0$ only (low/zero spin): ring galaxy — concentric density rings,
    no spiral arms. M31's star-formation rings are the axisymmetric SSV prediction.
    \item $m=2$ (moderate spin): grand design two-armed spiral (SA/SAB).
    \item $m=3$ (higher spin): three-armed grand design.
    \item $m=2+3+4$ superposition: flocculent multi-arm spiral (Sc/Sd).
    \item $m=2$, $N_\mathrm{rot} < 1$: open arms / bar (winding not yet complete).
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:morphology} shows the density field for each case,
generated from equation~\eqref{eq:density2d} with the pitch angle set by
equation~\eqref{eq:pitch}.
The entire Hubble sequence emerges from one equation and two observables:
$M_\mathrm{BH}$ (sets $k$ and $\varepsilon$) and $a_\mathrm{BH}$
(sets which modes are active).

\subsection{Corotation Radius and the Hubble--BH Mass Relation}

The corotation radius $r_c = m\mathcal{C}/(\pi M_\mathrm{BH})$ is predicted
with zero free parameters.
Since $r_c \propto M_\mathrm{BH}^{-1}$, more massive black holes have
\emph{smaller} corotation radii, meaning the disc completes more rotations
per unit time at $r_c$, winding the spiral more tightly:
\begin{align}
    \text{larger } M_\mathrm{BH}
    &\;\Longrightarrow\; \text{smaller } r_c
    \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{more rotations at } r_c \notag\\
    &\;\Longrightarrow\; \text{smaller pitch angle } \alpha
    \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{earlier Hubble type.}
    \label{eq:hubble_chain}
\end{align}
This is consistent with the observed anti-correlation between pitch angle
and black hole mass found by Seigar et al.~\cite{seigar2008},
who measured pitch angles for a sample of spirals with dynamically determined
$M_\mathrm{BH}$ and found exactly this trend.

For a sample of eight galaxies with known $M_\mathrm{BH}$ and published pitch
angles~\cite{seigar2008,davis2012}, the implied number of corotation orbits
$N_\mathrm{rot}$ ranges from 0.5 to 2.0, with a mean of $1.0\pm0.5$.
The mean pitch angle for $m=2$ spirals with $N_\mathrm{rot}=1$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \alpha \;=\; \arctan\!\left(\frac{2}{2\pi}\right)
    \;\approx\; 17.7°,
    \label{eq:alpha17}
\end{equation}
consistent with the observed median pitch angle of grand design spirals
($\sim15°$--$20°$~\cite{davis2012}).

The quantitative derivation of pitch angle from the SSV disc dispersion
relation is an open problem deferred to a companion calculation.

\section{Falsifiable Predictions}
\label{sec:predictions}
%%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Node spacing scales as $M_\mathrm{BH}^{-1}$.}
High-resolution ($\lesssim2\;\mathrm{kpc}$) HI surveys of galaxies with
$M_\mathrm{BH} \sim 10^7$--$10^8\,M_\odot$ should reveal quasi-periodic
velocity undulations with spacings matching Table~\ref{tab:predict} to
within $\pm20\%$.
Non-detection at predicted spacings in Centaurus~A and NGC~3198 would falsify
the model.

\item \textbf{Wiggle amplitude scales as $M_\mathrm{BH}$.}
The velocity wiggle $\delta v = \sqrt{G\pi/\mathcal{C}}\cdot M_\mathrm{BH}$
predicts $\delta v = 12.1\;\mathrm{km/s}$ for M31 and $4.8\;\mathrm{km/s}$
for Centaurus~A.
Measured amplitudes in 2D velocity residual maps (after tilted-ring subtraction)
should follow this scaling.

\item \textbf{HI density rings at predicted antinode radii.}
In face-on or moderately inclined galaxies, the HI surface density map should
show concentric overdense rings at $r = j_{0,n}/k$
($n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$), independent of the azimuthal arm structure.
For M31: rings at $\approx 9.8, 22.6, 35.4\;\mathrm{kpc}$.

\item \textbf{Hubble type anti-correlates with $M_\mathrm{BH}$ at fixed spin.}
In a volume-limited sample of late-type galaxies with measured $M_\mathrm{BH}$,
galaxies with more massive black holes should show tighter spiral arms
(earlier Hubble type) independent of other galaxy properties.
This extends the Seigar et al.~\cite{seigar2008} result to a statistical sample.

\item \textbf{No dark matter particle.}
All direct-detection, collider, and astrophysical searches for WIMPs, axions,
or sterile neutrinos should return null results.

\item \textbf{Andromeda satellite plane.}
The 30 co-rotating dwarf satellites of M31 lie on the first nodal surface of
the $m=0$ wave at $\approx 9.8\;\mathrm{kpc}$ above the disc plane,
with thickness $h \approx 14\;\mathrm{kpc}$ set by the vacuum healing length
(no free parameters).
\textit{Gaia}-class proper-motion surveys should confirm co-rotation and
perpendicular velocity dispersion $\sigma_\perp \approx 12\;\mathrm{km/s}$.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Relation to Superfluid Dark Matter}

The superfluid dark matter (SFDM) programme~\cite{berezhiani2015,khoury2015}
correctly identifies that a superfluid equation of state is needed on galactic
scales. The SSV shares this mathematical structure but differs in one crucial
respect: the superfluid is the vacuum, not a new particle.

SFDM has encountered observational difficulties: the model is in tension with
weak gravitational lensing at large radii~\cite{mistele2023} and produces
unnatural mass-to-light ratio trends across the galaxy size
distribution~\cite{mistele2022}.
These tensions arise because SFDM must simultaneously explain cosmological
structure (requiring cold dark matter behaviour) and galactic structure
(requiring superfluid behaviour) with the same particle.

The SSV avoids this tension entirely: cosmological structure is not attributed
to a dark matter halo but to the medium's own thermodynamic history
(addressed in the companion series).
The galactic results here — flat curves, wave structure, morphology — are
purely local consequences of the vacuum's equation of state and the CBH resonance.

\subsection{The Winding Problem}

The classical winding problem asks why spiral arms persist for many Gyr
despite differential rotation winding them to $\sim1°$ pitch in $<1\;\mathrm{Gyr}$.
The standard answer (Lin-Shu density wave theory) treats arms as waves,
not material streams.
The SSV provides the same answer but with a mechanical origin: arms are
soliton modes of the disc order parameter $\Psi$, continuously refreshed
by the CBH driving at $f_\mathrm{BH}$.
The pattern persists as long as the CBH oscillates — indefinitely,
in the superfluid limit.

\subsection{Open Problems}

Three open problems are acknowledged.

    analogue of the Lin-Shu dispersion relation for the LogSE equation of state

    The mode amplitudes $\varepsilon_m(a_\mathrm{BH})$ require the same analysis.
    The qualitative result (heavier BH $\to$ tighter arms, earlier Hubble type)
    is structural and does not depend on this derivation.

    \item \emph{First-principles derivation of $\mathcal{C}$.}
    The calibrated value $\mathcal{C} = 1.80\times10^9\;\mathrm{kpc}\cdot M_\odot$
    is reproduced by the dimensional combination
    $\hbar^2\alpha^{-16}(m_p/m_e)^7/(2Gm_e^2)$ at integer exponents,
    suggesting it is a physical constant of the medium.
    A derivation of these exponents as eigenvalues of the 2D LogSE disc soliton
    is identified as a companion calculation.

    \item \emph{Electroweak sector.}
    The SSV force sector (SSV~II) has a factor-of-eight discrepancy between
    the estimated and measured $W$ boson mass.\footnote{This discrepancy
    is unrelated to the galactic results and does not affect any prediction

    Resolving it requires a full 3D numerical computation of the
    vortex reconnection barrier in the Gross-Pitaevskii functional.
\end{enumerate}

    the corotation radius: more massive black holes wind their arms more tightly,

    \item \textbf{The winding problem is resolved}: arms are soliton modes
    refreshed by the CBH, not material streams that wind up.
\end{enumerate}
