\begin{abstract}
This paper treats black holes in the Saturated Superfluid Vacuum (SSV) framework as coherent
condensate objects rather than singular endpoints of collapse. In SSV, collapse terminates in a
finite condensate state of the vacuum medium. The horizon is interpreted as an acoustic
freezing surface: outward progress relative to the exterior tends to zero, but this does not
imply that the object is pinned or that angular momentum must vanish. Spin is treated as a
topological property of the condensate rather than as ordinary internal material rotation.

The paper also develops the claim that information is not destroyed in a singularity, because
there is no singularity in the first place. Instead, horizon structure functions as the
memory-bearing boundary of the condensate.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

This paper treats black holes in SSV as coherent condensate objects with frozen interiors,
topological spin, and finite structure.

The purpose is intentionally narrow. We do not attempt a full Kerr derivation, a galaxy-scale
standing-wave model, or a cosmological synthesis. Those belong to later or separate papers.
Here the question is ontological: what is a black hole if the vacuum is a saturated superfluid
medium rather than empty spacetime?

The answer proposed here is that gravitational collapse does not end in an infinite-density
singularity. It ends in a finite coherent condensate state of the vacuum medium. The horizon is
then not a material wall and not merely a coordinate artifact. It is the surface at which
outward acoustic progress relative to the exterior becomes frozen. This freezing constrains
propagating change; it does not erase topology, angular momentum, or horizon memory.

\section{Black Holes as Condensates}

In the condensate picture, a black hole is a finite saturated state of the same medium that
supports particles, phonons, and vortex structure elsewhere in the SSV program. Collapse
pushes the medium into an extreme coherent phase rather than into a mathematical point of
infinite density.

This distinction matters because it changes what must be explained. A classical singularity
asks how physical structure can survive at a point where curvature and density diverge. The
condensate picture instead asks what coherent state the medium approaches when ordinary
outward acoustic response is no longer available.

On this reading, the low average density of very massive black holes is not paradoxical. A
supermassive object need not be locally Planck-dense throughout its volume. Its defining
feature is not infinite density, but the formation of a horizon-scale coherent state whose
interior update channels are causally frozen relative to the exterior.

The claim is therefore modest but sharp:
\begin{quote}
A black hole is a finite condensate of the vacuum medium bounded by an acoustic-freezing
surface.
\end{quote}

\section{Horizon as Acoustic-Freezing Surface}

The word ``freezing'' can be misleading if it is read as ordinary mechanical pinning. In this
paper it has a specific meaning. The horizon is an acoustic-freezing surface because outward
propagating change, measured relative to exterior observers, loses the ability to carry
information back out.

This is not the claim that the condensate is nailed to space, nor that all internal structure
is literally motionless in an absolute sense. It is a claim about available outward update
channels. The horizon marks the boundary where escape-directed acoustic progress tends to
zero.

Thus:
\begin{itemize}
  \item freezing means the failure of outward signal propagation relative to the exterior,
  \item pinning would mean absolute spatial immobilization,
  \item the SSV black-hole ontology requires freezing, not pinning.
\end{itemize}

This distinction is essential for avoiding an apparent contradiction. If the horizon freezes
outward acoustic time, it does not follow that the black hole cannot move, carry spin, or store
boundary structure. Those are global and topological properties of the condensate, not ordinary
outward sound pulses escaping from the interior.

\section{Spin Without Acoustic Time}

A common objection is immediate: if time stops at the horizon, why does a black hole spin?

The answer is that spin is not being treated here as ordinary internal material rotation
requiring freely propagating acoustic time through the interior. In SSV, spin is a topological
property of the condensate state. It is carried by winding, circulation, and boundary
structure. A frozen horizon can suppress outward acoustic progress while still preserving
global angular momentum.

An analogy is a vortex in a superfluid. The circulation is not a little bead orbiting a track.
It is a topological organization of the phase field. Likewise, the spin of a condensate black
hole is not modeled as ordinary matter rotating inside a classical cavity after all local
clocks have stopped. It is the persistence of topological winding in a coherent medium state.

This also separates two claims that should not be conflated:
\begin{itemize}
  \item spin persistence: a black-hole condensate can carry angular momentum as topology,
  \item population claims: astrophysical black holes may or may not cluster near particular
  spin values.
\end{itemize}

Only the first claim belongs to this paper. Any claim about near-extremal spin populations
requires separate observational and dynamical support and is intentionally not used here.

\section{Information and Horizon Structure}

The information-loss problem is sharpened by singularity language. If collapse ends at a
point where ordinary physical structure is destroyed, then one must explain how information is
either preserved, emitted, or lost.

In the SSV condensate picture, there is no singular endpoint at which structure must vanish.
The horizon is instead a memory-bearing boundary of a finite coherent object. Infalling
structure changes the condensate and its boundary state. The exterior may lose access to the
fine interior organization, but that is not the same as physical annihilation of information.

This paper does not claim a completed microscopic entropy formula for the horizon. It claims a
different ontology for the storage problem:
\begin{quote}
Information is not destroyed in a singularity, because the black hole is not a singularity.
It is redistributed into condensate and horizon structure.
\end{quote}

This is consistent with the broader SSV view of entropy as wake-complexity: events are written
into present structure. For black holes, the relevant present structure is the horizon-scale
condensate boundary.

\section{Discussion and Open Problems}

The condensate picture is not a replacement for every successful calculation in general
relativity. It is an ontology and mechanism proposal that must reproduce the appropriate GR
limits where those limits are tested.

The correspondence claimed here is qualitative:
\begin{itemize}
  \item horizons are retained as real causal boundaries,
  \item escape-directed propagation fails at the horizon,
  \item black holes carry mass, angular momentum, and horizon memory,
  \item singularities are replaced by finite coherent condensate interiors.
\end{itemize}

The correspondence not claimed here is equally important:
\begin{itemize}
  \item no full Kerr metric derivation is provided,
  \item no black-hole population model is asserted,
  \item no galaxy-scale standing-wave application is made,
  \item no broad cosmology follows from this paper alone.
\end{itemize}

The next technical tasks are to derive the exterior metric limit, specify the horizon entropy
functional in SSV variables, and connect topological spin to the familiar angular-momentum
parameters used in relativistic black-hole phenomenology.

\section{Conclusion}

In SSV, black holes are finite coherent condensates whose horizons freeze outward progress but
do not erase topology, motion, or information-bearing boundary structure.
